## 1. Breathing Protocols

### 1.1 Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)

- **Parameters**: Inhale 4s — hold-full 4s — exhale 4s — hold-empty 4s. One cycle = 16s ([WebMD via box-breathing guide](https://www.medicinenet.com/why_do_navy_seals_use_box_breathing/article.htm)).
- **Rounds / duration**: Typically 2–3 minutes per session, 1–2× weekly is the dose Huberman Lab references; counts can be scaled (3-3-3-3 up to 6-6-6-6) based on the user's CO₂ tolerance ([Huberman Lab newsletter, Breathwork Protocols](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/breathwork-protocols-for-health-focus-stress)).
- **Origin / source**: Popularized by Navy SEAL trainer Mark Divine; also used by ER physicians and elite athletes for stress down-regulation ([US Navy Medicine — Combat Tactical Breathing PDF](https://www.med.navy.mil/Portals/62/Documents/NMFA/NMCPHC/root/Documents/health-promotion-wellness/psychological-emotional-wellbeing/Combat-Tactical-Breathing.pdf)).
- **Intended effect**: Stress reduction, focus, cortisol reduction, pre-performance calm; engages respiratory sinus arrhythmia and improves vagal tone at the slow ~5–6 bpm rate it produces ([Huberman Lab newsletter](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/breathwork-protocols-for-health-focus-stress)).

### 1.2 Tactical / Combat Breathing

- **Parameters**: Identical mechanics to box breathing (4-4-4-4 through the nose), but with explicit instruction not to clamp the glottis on the hold and to perform 3–6 cycles before high-stakes action or debrief ([US Navy Medicine PDF](https://www.med.navy.mil/Portals/62/Documents/NMFA/NMCPHC/root/Documents/health-promotion-wellness/psychological-emotional-wellbeing/Combat-Tactical-Breathing.pdf)).
- **Source**: US Navy / Marine Corps psychological-wellbeing protocol; also covered in *On Combat* (Grossman). Same protocol marketed by SOF units pre-mission.
- **Intended effect**: Acute autonomic reset under operational stress; preserves fine motor control and decision-making (claims based on military field experience; rigorous controlled trials are limited — see [PubMed 32757097](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32757097/)).

### 1.3 Triangle Breathing (4-4-4)

- **Parameters**: Inhale 4s — hold-full 4s — exhale 4s. No empty hold. Three-phase loop (a "triangle"), commonly used in pediatric and clinical anxiety settings ([St George's NHS — Triangle Breathing PDF](https://www.stgeorges.nhs.uk/wp-content/uploads/2020/04/SGH-WCFS-MDT-Triangle-Breathing.pdf)).
- **Rounds / duration**: 1–5 minutes; often 5–10 cycles for kids. Scales as 5-5-5 or 6-6-6.
- **Intended effect**: Lower-intensity alternative to box breathing; nervous-system regulation without the empty-lung hold, which some practitioners find more accessible for beginners and anxious users.

### 1.4 4-7-8 (Dr. Andrew Weil)

- **Parameters**: Tongue tip on the palatal ridge behind the upper front teeth (held throughout). Exhale fully through the mouth with an audible "whoosh." Close mouth, inhale through nose for 4. Hold for 7. Exhale through mouth for 8 (whoosh). The exhale phase is twice the inhale ([Cleveland Clinic — 4-7-8 Breathing](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/4-7-8-breathing); [WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/balance/what-to-know-4-7-8-breathing)).
- **Rounds**: Weil prescribes a hard cap of **4 cycles** for the first month; may extend to 8 cycles after that. Twice daily ([Cleveland Clinic](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/4-7-8-breathing)).
- **Source**: Dr. Andrew Weil, Arizona Center for Integrative Medicine; rooted in pranayama; taught since the 1990s ([WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/balance/what-to-know-4-7-8-breathing)).
- **Intended effect**: Parasympathetic activation, sleep onset, anxiety reduction. A 2022 sleep-deprivation study (PMC9277512) reported lower LF/HF ratio and increased HF power, consistent with greater vagal activity, after 4-7-8 practice ([PMC9277512](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9277512/)).

### 1.5 Wim Hof Method (WHM)

- **Parameters** (one round):
  1. 30–40 deep, full breaths through nose or mouth (inhale fully, passive exhale — *no forced full exhale*).
  2. After the last breath, exhale and **hold on empty lungs** as long as possible (retention).
  3. Inhale fully and hold on full lungs for ~15 seconds ("recovery breath").
- **Rounds**: Typically 3–4 rounds; session length 15–20 min ([Medical News Today](https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/wim-hof-breathing-method); [Nature Sci Rep 2023, s41598-023-44902-0](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-44902-0)).
- **Retention progression in study protocols**: round 1 ~30s, round 2 ~60s, round 3 ~90s ([PMC8424088 Wim Hof pilot study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8424088/)).
- **Intended effect**: Adrenaline release, transient alkalosis (pH rises during hyperventilation), drop in SpO₂ during retention, anti-inflammatory effects via endogenous cortisol/adrenaline, subjective alertness and mood lift ([Nature Sci Rep 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-44902-0); [PLoS One Wim Hof review](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0286933&type=printable)).
- **NB**: Huberman Lab labels this category "cyclic hyperventilation" and uses 25 breaths × up to 5 min total with 15–30s empty holds ([Huberman Lab](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/breathwork-protocols-for-health-focus-stress)). See safety section — significant contraindications.

### 1.6 Coherent / Resonance Breathing (~5.5 bpm)

- **Parameters**: Equal inhale and exhale, no holds. ~5.5 breaths/min = inhale 5.5s, exhale 5.5s (≈11s cycle). Individual resonance frequency varies 4.5–7.0 bpm ([Frontiers in Neuroscience — Practical Guide to RF Assessment, PMC7578229](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7578229/)).
- **Duration**: 10–20 min/day; 4-week protocols show measurable HRV gains ([PMC5575449](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5575449/)).
- **Source**: Stephen Elliott (popularized "Coherent Breathing"); HRV biofeedback literature (Lehrer, Vaschillo).
- **Intended effect**: Maximal HRV amplitude at ~0.1 Hz, baroreflex engagement, increased RMSSD (vagal tone marker), reduced blood pressure ([PMC5575449](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5575449/); [PMC8924557](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8924557/)).

### 1.7 Extended Exhale (e.g., 4-8, 6-3-6-3)

- **Parameters (6-3-6-3)**: Inhale 6s — hold 3s — exhale 6s — hold-empty 3s. One cycle = 18s.
- **Parameters (4-8 / 4-6-8)**: Inhale 4s, exhale 6–8s. No holds required.
- **Source / evidence**: Slow breathing review (PMC10395759) confirms exhale-extended ratios reduce physiological arousal more than equal-ratio breathing ([PMC10395759 — Slow breathing review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10395759/)).
- **Intended effect**: Parasympathetic dominance via prolonged vagal afferent stimulation during exhale.

### 1.8 Pranayama

#### 1.8a Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril)

- **Parameters**: Sit upright. Close right nostril with right thumb, inhale through left nostril (~4s). Close left nostril with ring finger, release right thumb, exhale through right (~4–8s). Inhale right, switch, exhale left. That's one cycle ([Healthline — Alternate Nostril Breathing](https://www.healthline.com/health/alternate-nostril-breathing)).
- **Duration**: 5–12 min/day; the cognitive-improvement RCT used 12 min/day × 4 weeks (Int J Yoga 2013, summarized in [WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/balance/what-to-know-about-alternate-nostril-breathing)).
- **Intended effect**: Lowered resting HR, reduced systolic BP (especially in pre-hypertensives), improved HRV, sustained-attention gains ([Asian J Med Sci 2024](https://ajmsjournal.info/index.php/AJMS/article/view/4313)).

#### 1.8b Kapalabhati ("Skull Shining")

- **Parameters**: Forceful active exhale via sharp abdominal contraction; passive recoil inhale. Rate: ~60–120 exhalations/min (1–2 Hz). Beginners: 15 cycles, build to longer rounds ([PMC8963645 — Kapalbhati autonomic study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8963645/)).
- **Rounds**: 3 rounds of 30–60 breaths is a common intermediate dose; rest 30–60s between rounds.
- **Intended effect**: Sympathetic activation during practice, parasympathetic rebound after; CO₂ washout, increased gamma EEG activity post-practice ([PMC8963645](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8963645/)).

#### 1.8c Bhastrika ("Bellows")

- **Parameters**: Forceful active inhale **and** active exhale through the nose, equal force on both phases. Two documented rates:
  - **Slow Bhastrika**: ~6 breaths/min, with ~4s inhale + ~6s exhale ([PubMed 19249921](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19249921/)).
  - **Fast Bhastrika**: 60–120 breaths/min in fast variants ([PMC8963645](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8963645/)).
- **Rounds / duration**: Beginners 10–15 breaths/round; clinical study protocols use 5 min sessions, or 10–15 min × 6 days/week × 4–6 weeks ([PMC7253694 — Bhastrika RCT on anxiety](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7253694/)).
- **Intended effect**: Anxiety reduction; altered brain functional connectivity (anterior insula, amygdala) in the Bhastrika RCT; energizing ([PMC7253694](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7253694/)).

#### 1.8d Ujjayi ("Ocean Breath" / Victorious Breath)

- **Parameters**: Slight glottal constriction during nasal inhale and exhale, producing an audible ocean-like sound. Common ratio 4 in : 6 out (exhale ~50% longer). Cycle ~10s → ~6 bpm ([Yogabasics — Ujjayi Pranayama](https://www.yogabasics.com/practice/ujjayi-pranayama/)).
- **Duration**: 10–15 min standalone, or sustained throughout an asana practice.
- **Intended effect**: Resonance-zone breathing rate yields HRV/BP benefits similar to coherent breathing; vagal engagement via glottal/laryngeal control ([clinicaltrials.gov NCT06663800](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT06663800)). Note: focused clinical research on Ujjayi alone is limited compared to other pranayama.

### 1.9 Buteyko Breathing

- **Parameters**: Lifestyle-level "reduced breathing" — train nasal-only breathing, minimize breath volume, periodic small breath-holds. Core practice block: comfortable nasal breath in (~3–4s), out (~3–5s), pause until mild air hunger; repeat 3–4 min ([Buteyko Clinic International — Control Pause guide](https://buteykoclinic.com/blogs/news/how-test-your-breathing-breath-hold-control-pause)).
- **Source**: Konstantin Buteyko (1950s, USSR); popularized clinically for asthma.
- **Intended effect**: Raise CO₂ tolerance, reduce hyperventilation, improve asthma control. Asthma efficacy is the best-evidenced application — see asthma RCT ([ClinicalTrials NCT05390554](https://clinicaltrials.gov/study/NCT05390554)).

### 1.10 Physiological Sigh / Cyclic Sighing (Huberman)

- **Parameters**: Inhale deeply through nose → second sharp top-up inhale through nose (no exhale between) → long slow exhale through mouth until empty.
- **Dosing**:
  - Acute reset: 1–3 sighs.
  - Daily protocol (Spiegel/Huberman/Yilmaz Balban): **5 minutes/day** of cyclic sighing for 4 weeks ([Cell Reports Medicine 2023, S2666-3791(22)00474-8](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/pdf/S2666-3791(22)00474-8.pdf); [Stanford Medicine summary](https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2023/02/cyclic-sighing-can-help-breathe-away-anxiety.html)).
- **Source**: Mechanism described in Feldman lab (UCLA) work; clinical trial co-led by Spiegel (Stanford) and Huberman (Stanford) ([Stanford Medicine](https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2023/02/cyclic-sighing-can-help-breathe-away-anxiety.html)).
- **Intended effect**: Reinflates collapsed alveoli, offloads CO₂, parasympathetic activation. The Stanford RCT (n=111) found cyclic sighing produced the **greatest positive-affect improvement** (~1.91 pts/day) of the four arms tested — beating box breathing, cyclic hyperventilation, and mindfulness meditation; effect compounded with consecutive days ([Cell Reports Medicine 2023](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/pdf/S2666-3791(22)00474-8.pdf)).

## 2. Physiology — What Each Protocol Actually Does

**Vagal tone / parasympathetic activation.** Slow breathing at ~5–6 bpm with exhale ≥ inhale activates vagal afferents during the exhale; this is the mechanism behind coherent breathing, Ujjayi, 4-7-8, extended exhale, and cyclic sighing. Heart rate falls during exhale (respiratory sinus arrhythmia) — pacing breath at the resonance frequency maximizes the amplitude of this oscillation ([PMC5575449](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5575449/); [PMC7578229](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7578229/)).

**HRV and the baroreflex.** Breathing at ~0.1 Hz (≈6 bpm) creates resonance between respiratory, cardiac, and baroreflex oscillators. Time-domain HRV markers (SDNN, RMSSD) and the baroreflex sensitivity index all rise during paced breathing at this rate, and trained users show carryover gains in resting HRV ([PMC5575449](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5575449/); [PMC12082064 — HRV biofeedback review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12082064/)).

**CO₂ tolerance.** The urge to breathe is driven primarily by CO₂ chemoreception (not low O₂). Reduced-breathing methods (Buteyko, exhale-emphasis, retention work) raise the user's CO₂ set point, leading to lower resting respiratory rate and the perception that breathing is "easier." Tracked via BOLT score ([Buteyko Clinic guide](https://buteykoclinic.com/blogs/news/how-test-your-breathing-breath-hold-control-pause)).

**Hyperventilation / cyclic hyperventilation (WHM, Kapalabhati, Bhastrika fast).** Drives CO₂ down, blood pH up (respiratory alkalosis), peripheral vasoconstriction (cerebral included), and sympathetic activation with adrenaline release. The breath-hold that follows then drops SpO₂ but the delayed urge-to-breathe (because CO₂ is low) is what makes the long retention possible — and what makes this category most dangerous in water ([Nature Sci Rep 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-023-44902-0); [Live Science — experts on Wim Hof dangers](https://www.livescience.com/health/gambling-with-your-life-experts-weigh-in-on-dangers-of-the-wim-hof-method)).

**Alveolar reinflation.** Sighs (single or cyclic) re-open collapsed alveoli — this is the homeostatic role sighs evolved to play (Feldman lab). The double-inhale specifically maximizes alveolar recruitment before the long exhale offloads accumulated CO₂ ([Stanford Medicine](https://med.stanford.edu/news/insights/2023/02/cyclic-sighing-can-help-breathe-away-anxiety.html)).

**Nitric oxide / nasal breathing.** Nasal passages produce nitric oxide that dilates pulmonary vessels and improves gas exchange. Most pranayama and Buteyko explicitly enforce nasal-only inhale/exhale for this reason ([Huberman Lab](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/breathwork-protocols-for-health-focus-stress)).

## 3. Performance / Fitness Tests

### 3.1 BOLT Score (Body Oxygen Level Test / Control Pause)

**Protocol** ([Buteyko Clinic](https://buteykoclinic.com/blogs/news/how-test-your-breathing-breath-hold-control-pause); [Oxygen Advantage](https://oxygenadvantage.com/blogs/science/measure-bolt)):
1. Sit upright, breathe normally for 2–3 min.
2. Take a **normal, gentle nasal inhale**, then a normal nasal exhale.
3. At the bottom of the exhale, pinch the nose. Start a timer.
4. Stop the timer at the **first definite urge to breathe** (involuntary swallow, diaphragm contraction, throat tightening) — NOT the maximum hold.
5. The first breath after release should be calm and through the nose; if you gasp, you held too long — invalid test.

**Interpretation scale** ([Oxygen Advantage; multiple coach sites](https://oxygenadvantage.com/blogs/science/measure-bolt)):
| BOLT (s) | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| <10 | Severely dysfunctional breathing |
| 10–20 | Significant dysfunctional breathing |
| 20–30 | Functional but with room to improve |
| 30–40 | Good — approaching optimal |
| 40+ | Excellent / optimal breathing |

**Measurement hygiene**: Same time of day (morning, on waking, before coffee). Day-to-day variation of ±2s is noise; look at 10-day or 4-week trends ([Buteyko Clinic](https://buteykoclinic.com/blogs/news/how-test-your-breathing-breath-hold-control-pause)).

### 3.2 CO₂ Tolerance Test (CO₂TT) — Freediving Variant

**Protocol** ([Breathe Your Truth — CO2TT](https://breatheyourtruth.com/carbon-dioxide-tolerance-test-co2tt/); [DeeperBlue freediving forum](https://forums.deeperblue.com/threads/the-one-minute-co2-tolerance-test.42156/)):
1. Three calm nasal breaths to baseline.
2. One full inhale.
3. Start timer at the *beginning* of exhale.
4. Exhale through pursed lips as **slowly, smoothly, and quietly as possible**. The timer stops when you can't exhale any further.
5. Score = duration of the controlled exhale in seconds.

**Interpretation** (common freediving rubric):
- <20s: poor CO₂ tolerance.
- 20–40s: average.
- 40–60s: good.
- 60–80s: very good.
- 80s+: elite / trained freediver.

Different from BOLT — CO₂TT measures controlled-exhale endurance, BOLT measures breath-hold-after-exhale to first urge. CO₂TT is more breath-volume-dependent.

### 3.3 Maximum Breath Hold (Static Apnea)

**Protocol**:
1. Rest seated/supine for 2–5 min, normal breathing.
2. Take a full inhale (≈100% of vital capacity).
3. Pinch nose, start timer, hold until break point (involuntary gasp).
4. Recovery breaths after.

**Normative values** ([PubMed 7995705](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/7995705/)):
- Healthy non-smoking adults: ~38–56s (mean ~45s).
- Smokers / COPD / CHF: 18–32s (mean ~25s).
- Casual users typically 25–30s.
- Elite freedivers: 6–10+ minutes (highly trained, not a target for general users).

**Safety**: Should only be performed seated/supine on dry land, never in or near water (see §4).

### 3.4 HRV-Based Metrics

**Resting HRV (RMSSD)**: Root mean square of successive RR-interval differences; vagally mediated. Measured supine or seated, 1–5 min via chest strap or photoplethysmography (Apple Watch, Oura, Whoop, Polar H10). RMSSD rises with parasympathetic activity, falls with stress/sleep debt/illness ([PMC12082064](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12082064/)).

**Resonance frequency assessment**: Have the user paced-breathe at 4.5, 5.0, 5.5, 6.0, 6.5, 7.0 bpm for 2 min each; record HRV (LF power, peak-to-trough amplitude). The rate that maximizes HRV amplitude **is** the user's RF. This is the gold standard for HRV biofeedback training ([Frontiers — Practical Guide to RF Assessment, PMC7578229](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7578229/)).

**Practice-effect tracking**: Pre/post-session RMSSD delta is a useful in-app metric if the user has a paired wearable. 4-week paced-breathing protocols show measurable resting RMSSD gains ([PMC5575449](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5575449/)).

## 4. Safety Contraindications

Aggregated from breathwork-safety sources and clinical reviews; intense hyperventilation protocols carry more risk than slow/coherent protocols.

### 4.1 Hard Contraindications (do not practice intense breathwork without medical clearance)

- **Pregnancy** — particularly first trimester. CO₂/O₂ shifts and sympathetic surges from cyclic hyperventilation are not safe for the fetus. Gentle slow-paced breathing (coherent, slow exhale) is generally pregnancy-compatible ([BreathSesh — Wim Hof Safety](https://breathsesh.app/blog/wim-hof-safety-contraindications); [9D Breathwork contraindications](https://9dbreathwork.com/9d-breathwork-contraindications-pregnancy-anxiety-heart/)).
- **Epilepsy / seizure disorders** — hyperventilation is a known clinical seizure trigger; CO₂ drop alters cortical excitability ([BreathSesh](https://breathsesh.app/blog/wim-hof-safety-contraindications); [Unity Breathwork — contraindications glossary](https://unitybreathwork.com/breathwork-contraindications/)).
- **Cardiovascular disease** — uncontrolled hypertension, recent MI, history of stroke or aneurysm, arrhythmias. Adrenaline release and BP swings during cyclic hyperventilation can be unsafe ([BreathSesh](https://breathsesh.app/blog/wim-hof-safety-contraindications); [Unity Breathwork](https://unitybreathwork.com/breathwork-contraindications/)).
- **Glaucoma / retinal detachment** — intra-thoracic and intra-cranial pressure changes during retention can raise intra-ocular pressure ([Holotropic Breathwork LA — contraindications](https://www.holotropicbreathworkla.com/contraindications)).
- **Severe psychiatric conditions** (active psychosis, severe PTSD without therapeutic supervision, severe panic disorder) — intense breathwork can trigger or worsen episodes ([Unity Breathwork](https://unitybreathwork.com/breathwork-contraindications/); [Holotropic Breathwork LA](https://www.holotropicbreathworkla.com/contraindications)).

### 4.2 Absolute Water Rule (Wim Hof / cyclic hyperventilation)

- **NEVER perform cyclic hyperventilation in or near water** — bath, shower, pool, ice bath, ocean. The hyperventilation suppresses the CO₂-driven urge to breathe, so a practitioner can lose consciousness underwater **before** any "I need to breathe" signal arrives. Australian pathology researchers have specifically identified WHM as a practice associated with underwater breath-holding deaths ([Live Science](https://www.livescience.com/health/gambling-with-your-life-experts-weigh-in-on-dangers-of-the-wim-hof-method); [BreathSesh](https://breathsesh.app/blog/wim-hof-safety-contraindications)).
- **NEVER perform while driving or operating machinery** ([Huberman Lab](https://www.hubermanlab.com/newsletter/breathwork-protocols-for-health-focus-stress)).
- **Always practice seated or supine** — fainting during retention is "not uncommon" in early practice ([WebMD — Wim Hof](https://www.webmd.com/balance/wim-hof-breathing-technique)).

### 4.3 Expected Side Effects (not dangerous, but worth warning the user)

- **Tingling / paresthesia** in fingers, toes, face during/after cyclic hyperventilation — caused by hypocapnia-induced calcium-binding changes; resolves on resumption of normal breathing ([WebMD](https://www.webmd.com/balance/wim-hof-breathing-technique)).
- **Lightheadedness / dizziness** — sit or lie down; should resolve in <60s.
- **Emotional release** — crying, anxiety, euphoria. Common in extended sessions, especially holotropic-style.

### 4.4 Per-Protocol Risk Map

| Protocol | Risk level | Primary risk |
|---|---|---|
| Coherent / 4-7-8 / Triangle / Nadi Shodhana / Ujjayi | Very low | Mild lightheadedness if overdone |
| Box breathing / Tactical | Low | Empty-lung hold can feel intense for new users |
| Buteyko reduced breathing | Low | Air hunger discomfort |
| Kapalabhati / Bhastrika | Moderate | Dizziness, BP spikes; avoid in pregnancy/hypertension |
| Wim Hof / cyclic hyperventilation | High | Syncope, water/driving deaths, contraindicated in pregnancy/epilepsy/CV disease |

## 5. Sound / Audio Cues

### 5.1 What existing apps do (product-team reference)

- **Breathwrk**: Timed inhale/hold/exhale with paired music, voice guidance, and haptics; user can customize voice/sound/haptics independently per exercise ([Breathwrk site](https://www.breathwrk.com/)). Short, "prescriptive" exercise format.
- **Othership**: Music-driven, facilitator voice over the top; 500+ guided sessions organized into UP / DOWN / ALL AROUND / BODY / BRAIN categories. The music itself is the pacing cue — production-grade tracks scored to the breath rhythm ([Othership app page](https://www.othership.us/app)).
- **Calm**: Embedded breathing tool inside a meditation/sleep app, gentle voice and ambient sound; pacing via visual orb + soft chime ([Othership comparison roundup](https://www.othership.us/resources/breathwork-apps)).
- **Open**: Combines breathwork with meditation and movement; voice-led sessions with ambient soundscapes ([Open Meditation 4-7-8 guide](https://o-p-e-n.com/blog/4-7-8-breathwork-technique)).
- **Inhale**: Habit-tracker-first app, simple paced sounds + streak metrics ([Inhale app](https://getinhale.app/)).

### 5.2 Common audio-cue patterns observed across breathing apps

- **Phase tones**: a soft "in" tone (rising pitch) and "out" tone (falling pitch) marking inhale/exhale transitions.
- **Tick / metronome** for box-style protocols at 1 Hz with the count.
- **Voice count**: "inhale 2, 3, 4 — hold 2, 3, 4 — exhale 2, 3, 4."
- **Bell / chime** at the start of each round and at session end.
- **Ambient bed**: ocean, rain, low drones — masks environmental noise and supports entrainment.
- **Haptics-paired**: short buzz on inhale start, longer buzz on exhale start (Breathwrk pattern).

### 5.3 Entrainment research

- **Binaural beats**: A 2023 PLOS One systematic review (PMC10198548) of 14 studies on brainwave entrainment found **mixed and inconsistent** results — 5 supported the entrainment hypothesis, 8 contradicted it, 1 mixed ([PMC10198548](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10198548/)). Bottom line: binaural beats may help some users feel calmer/more focused, but the claim that they reliably "drive" brain rhythms isn't well supported. Treat as a UX feature, not a medical claim.
- **Music tempo as pacing aid**: Slow, ~0.1 Hz (6 bpm) music phrasing or drone modulation can naturally pace breath without explicit cues — the Othership approach.
- **What's well-supported**: Combining audio + visual cues (e.g., expanding circle plus tone) produces the most intuitive entrainment experience for paced breathing apps (industry consensus in app-design literature, weaker primary evidence — flag as design heuristic, not clinical claim).

## 6. Progression Metrics

### 6.1 Compliance / habit metrics

- **Streak (consecutive days)** — strong loss-aversion lever; well-documented behavioral effect in habit-app literature. Caveat: a streak detached from outcome metrics is a vanity number ([Othership — best breathwork apps roundup](https://www.othership.us/resources/breathwork-apps)).
- **Total minutes practiced** (lifetime, weekly, monthly).
- **Sessions completed** by protocol type.
- **Time-of-day distribution** — morning vs. evening practice patterns; helpful for personalization.

### 6.2 Physiological / efficacy metrics (the ones that matter)

- **BOLT score trend** — weekly/biweekly measurement. The only at-home measure of CO₂ tolerance / breathing efficiency that doesn't require lab kit. Track 4-week trend, not day-to-day ([Buteyko Clinic](https://buteykoclinic.com/blogs/news/how-test-your-breathing-breath-hold-control-pause)).
- **CO₂TT (controlled exhale)** — complementary to BOLT for users who like freediving framing.
- **Max breath hold (static apnea)** — for users explicitly training retention (Wim Hof users, freedivers).
- **Deepest retention per session** for protocols with retention rounds (round 1, 2, 3 retention seconds).
- **Resting HRV (RMSSD)** via wearable integration — most objective marker of carryover into baseline autonomic state ([PMC12082064](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12082064/)).
- **Pre/post session HRV delta** — short-term reactivity check if the user has a chest strap or Apple Watch.
- **Resting respiratory rate** — Stanford cyclic-sighing study showed daily 5-min practice lowered resting respiratory rate, indicating a more parasympathetic baseline ([Cell Reports Medicine 2023](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/pdf/S2666-3791(22)00474-8.pdf)).

### 6.3 Subjective metrics

- **Mood / state ratings** pre and post session (1–5 scale, or PANAS short form). The Stanford study used positive-affect scales and showed cyclic sighing produced ~1.91 pts/day improvement ([Cell Reports Medicine 2023](https://www.cell.com/cell-reports-medicine/pdf/S2666-3791(22)00474-8.pdf)).
- **Perceived stress / anxiety** — periodic PSS-4 check-in.
- **Sleep onset latency** if user practices before sleep (especially 4-7-8 use case).

### 6.4 Recommended progression hierarchy for BreathFlow

1. **Tier 1 — Universal**: Streak, total minutes, sessions completed, mood delta. Available for every user from day 1.
2. **Tier 2 — In-app physiological**: BOLT score (weekly prompt), max retention per session (for protocols that include retention). No hardware needed.
3. **Tier 3 — Wearable-integrated**: Resting RMSSD trend, pre/post HRV delta, resting respiratory rate. Opt-in via Apple Health / Oura / Whoop / Polar integration.
4. **Tier 4 — Advanced**: Resonance frequency assessment (a 12-min guided test producing the user's personal optimal breathing rate). Differentiating feature; well-grounded in HRV biofeedback literature ([PMC7578229](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7578229/)).
